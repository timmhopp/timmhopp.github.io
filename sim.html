Simulating Autonomous and Human-Driven Vehicles

Course Project 2025/26 Spatial Simulation

Overview

This project explores how autonomous vehicles (AVs) and human-driven cars behave in a shared road network and whether AVs can reduce traffic congestion. Using the GAMA simulation platform, I implemented a simplified urban road network with traffic lights and different driver behaviors to compare three scenarios: only human drivers, only autonomous vehicles, and mixed traffic.

The simulation tracks average travel time, number of completed trips, and vehicle speeds over time to assess how coordination, communication, and driving style influence congestion and throughput.

Motivation

Traffic congestion causes massive economic and environmental costs. In the EU, traffic jams are estimated to cost hundreds of billions of euros per year, while cities like Salzburg lose significant time to congestion and stop-and-go traffic. Beyond time loss, exhaust fumes contribute to climate change and poor air quality.

The project is framed within the context of the UN Sustainable Development Goals, especially Sustainable Cities and Communities (SDG 11) and Climate Action (SDG 13), as well as the EU’s “Sustainable and Smart Mobility Strategy”. It asks whether smarter mobility—particularly autonomous vehicles—can be part of a realistic solution.

Research question

The central questions of the project are:

  • Can the use of autonomous vehicles reduce congestion compared to human-driven traffic?
  • Can this be effectively modeled in GAMA using a simplified road network?
  • How do average travel time, throughput, and speed differ between traffic compositions?

An autonomous vehicle is defined here as a vehicle that communicates with other AVs and traffic lights to optimize speed and routing, minimizing travel time and avoiding congestion.

Scenarios

All scenarios use the same road network, traffic light logic, and evaluation metrics. Only the driver type composition changes:

  1. Only Human Drivers: All vehicles follow human-like, noisy behavior.
  2. Only Autonomous Vehicles: All vehicles are AVs with coordinated behavior.
  3. Mixed Traffic: Half human-driven, half autonomous vehicles.

Road network and agent interactions

The initial idea was to derive the street network from OpenStreetMap, but this proved too complex for the scope of the project. Instead, I implemented a simplified, yet expressive network built from basic geometries:

  • Two-lane segments: Roads with two lanes per direction.
  • Single-lane segments: Roads with one lane per direction.
  • Start and end nodes: Entry and exit points for vehicles.
  • Traffic lights: Control intersections and communicate with AVs.

Agents interact through:

  • Cars ↔ Lanes: Lanes assign positions and directions to vehicles.
  • Cars ↔ Traffic lights: Human drivers react to visible signals; AVs also use communicated timing.
  • AV ↔ AV: Autonomous vehicles share position and speed information (V2V) to coordinate.

Driving behaviors

Human drivers vs. autonomous vehicles

Aspect Human Drivers Autonomous Vehicles
Speed Variable (±40%), random braking Consistent, smooth speeds
Distraction 2–8% chance each step Never distracted
Tailgating response Brake checks, blocking, “rage” behavior Yielding, defensive mode
Signal behavior May run reds, accelerate on yellow Signal-aware timing, never runs red
Communication Honking only (aggressive) V2V data sharing
Routing Fixed route Smart rerouting around congestion
Coordination None (competitive) Platooning, collective knowledge

These behavioral differences are encoded in GAMA as separate species with distinct decision rules, perception ranges, and reactions to nearby vehicles and signals.

Results

Human-driven only

In a scenario with 50 human-driven cars over 5000 simulation steps:

  • 185 trips were completed.
  • Average of ~85 steps per trip.
  • Average speed around ~1.5 units/step.
  • Speeds degrade over time and completed trips drop in later timesteps.

Autonomous only

In a scenario with 50 autonomous vehicles over 5000 steps:

  • 319 trips were completed.
  • Average of ~81 steps per trip.
  • Average speed around ~3 units/step.
  • Speeds and trip completions remain stable due to controlled maximum speeds and coordination.

Mixed traffic

With 25 human-driven and 25 autonomous vehicles:

  • AVs are faster on average and complete more trips.
  • AVs take less time per route and maintain smoother traffic flow.
  • Human-driven cars show higher speed peaks but more instability and congestion.

Overall, the simulations indicate that autonomous vehicles can help reduce congestion and improve traffic conditions, especially when they can coordinate and communicate with each other and with infrastructure.

Implementation details and challenges

The model was implemented in GAMA, starting from a simple geometric network after attempts to directly use OSM data proved too complex. I iteratively refined:

  • Traffic light logic: Ensuring correct stopping behavior and realistic timing.
  • Perception zones: Action areas around cars to detect others and react.
  • Performance: Balancing agent complexity with simulation speed.
  • Documentation: Recording assumptions, behaviors, and limitations.

Generative AI (GitHub Copilot / Claude) was used selectively to support coding, but all core modeling decisions and logic were designed and validated manually.
