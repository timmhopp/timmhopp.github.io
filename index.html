MSc Thesis: Multi-Scale Spatiotemporal Origin-Destination Traffic Flow Prediction

Master's Thesis 2026 Deep Learning

Abstract

Accurate forecasting of urban traffic is essential for mobility management, urban planning, and intelligent transportation systems but the strong spatial and temporal dependencies and complexities make it a difficult task. This thesis proposes a deep learning method that models traffic as a dynamic graph and models short-term patterns and long-term trends simultaneously. The architecture combines a Graph Attention Network with a two-fold temporal learning approach. Parallel Gated Recursive Units are used to learn rapid traffic changes on a short time frame, while a Long Short-Term Memory Recurrent Neural Network captures evolving daily patterns.

The model is evaluated on real-world taxi data from 2024 in New York City. Data was aggregated as origin-destination matrices and represented as a dynamic graph. Experiments are conducted for next-step predictions and long-term forecasting across horizons between six hours and seven days. The model achieves consistently accurate predictions, with single-step MAE between 2.23 and 2.69 and an RMSE of 3.58 to 4.90 and cosine similarities of 0.88 to 0.98. Longer forecasts into the future yield similar results with long-term MAE of 2.53 to 2.77, RMSE of 4.31 to 4.76, and consistent cosine similarities around 0.88. A systemic bias towards underprediction is found for short-term forecasts but shown as correctable through simple linear regression based on residuals. The results demonstrate that the model is capable of successfully forecasting traffic at different scales, preserving spatial structures and temporal dependencies.

Research Question

For evaluation, three key aspects are focused on in regards to traffic patterns and volumes prediction:

  1. Short-term performance: How well does the model capture rapid, short-term changes in traffic?
  2. Long-term performance: How accurately does the model learn and represent trends over longer time spans?
  3. Baseline comparison: Does the model provide improved predictions compared to statistical baseline methods?

Background

This project is part of my Master's Thesis on predicting long-term traffic patterns. The model combines a Graph Attention Network (GAT) with a two-branch temporal learning structure, splitting short-term and long-term predictions between multiple Graph Recurrent Units (GRUs) and a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network. Temporal Attention modules are used for refined learning of patterns over time.

The model takes windows of data up to time t and predicts a single Origin-Destination (OD) matrix at horizon length h. This is a novel approach, as predictions of similarly long time spans are absent from existing literature.

Spatio-temporal patterns are learned on a graph structure where traffic is modeled as weighted edges between fixed graph vertices derived from the geographic structure of the area of interest. The graph is processed as G = (V, Et, A), where V are the vertices, Et the amount of traffic, and A the adjacency matrix.

Model Structure

The model consists of three main components:

  • Graph Attention Network (GAT) for spatial attention from graph, adjacency, and distance
  • Long-Term Branch featuring an LSTM and a Temporal Attention Mechanism
  • Short-Term Branch with multiple GRUs and Temporal Attention Mechanisms

OD matrices are loaded from the tensor, each representing network traffic within fixed 10-minute timeframes. These are subsequently processed by the GAT and spatial attention is encoded. Temporal modules learn traffic over time from outputs of the GAT. GRUs receive subsets of the data processed by the LSTM, each training on repeating daily segments.

Temporal Learning

The Long-Term Branch receives the spatial encodings from the GAT and aggregates them, transforming them into a single vector for each time step through mean pooling. An LSTM is trained to learn daily patterns by processing a full day's data. The temporal embeddings are then forwarded for temporal attention learning.

Decoder

The decoder fuses the long-term and short-term context vectors through linear projections and non-linear transformations. A 3×3 convolution kernel is applied, and Softplus activation ensures non-negative values in the final OD matrix prediction.

Loss Function

The model uses the Negative Binomial negative log-likelihood (NB-NLL) loss function. OD traffic consists of non-negative integer counts and shows strong overdispersion, making the Negative Binomial distribution appropriate. The dispersion parameter is adapted to progressively reduce variance and remove noise.

Experiments

The model was tested on TLC Taxi Data for NYC for the year 2024. Data was filtered to contain only trips within the Borough of Manhattan. To further decrease complexity, zones were grouped based on total trip counts, adjacency, and distance between zones.

Adjacency and distance matrices were first calculated based on the provided Shapefile, then refactored using the clusters. Trips in OD matrices were summed per time step. The result was a 10×10 tensor.

The model showed excellent performance across all prediction horizons with minimal degradation over time, demonstrating that it successfully learned spatiotemporal patterns and generalized them effectively.

Key Findings

  • The multi-step model outperformed the single-step baseline across all metrics
  • Minimal error degradation even at very long horizons (1008 time steps = 7 days)
  • High cosine similarity (>0.87) maintained across all prediction horizons
  • Successfully captured both spatial relationships and temporal patterns at multiple scales
  • Demonstrated robust generalization to unseen traffic patterns
